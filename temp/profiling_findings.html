Raw Data Profiling — Findings¶

DSA Statement of Reasons data · TikTok vs X

This notebook walks through every key finding from the column quality check, grouped by theme. All numbers are derived from temp/profiling_output/.

Coverage: TikTok = sample of 20 files (15.4M rows, Jan–Nov 2025) out of 1,197 files / 51.5 GB.
X = full dataset (670k rows, 43 MB).

    sub = topn(df, col)

Interpretation.

  • Monetary decision fields (decision_monetary, decision_monetary_other, end_date_monetary_restriction) are 100% empty in both platforms — neither TikTok nor X issued any monetary restrictions in this period.
  • End-date fields (end_date_visibility_restriction, end_date_service_restriction, end_date_account_restriction) are effectively null everywhere, indicating that restrictions are applied without a stated end date — i.e., permanent or indefinite.
  • TikTok's decision_account is 99.34% null: account-level actions are rare; almost all decisions target content.
  • X's decision_account is 72.63% null — account actions (suspensions, terminations) are far more central to X's enforcement than TikTok's.

3 Decision Ground — The Biggest Structural Difference¶

The decision_ground field encodes whether a decision was taken for illegal content or incompatible (policy-violating) content.
This is one of the most important columns for comparative analysis — and the two platforms are polar opposites.

Finding.

  • TikTok: 99.99% of decisions are DECISION_GROUND_INCOMPATIBLE_CONTENT — own platform policy violations. Only 0.01% are for illegal content under EU law.
  • X: 100% of decisions are DECISION_GROUND_ILLEGAL_CONTENT — every single entry cites an EU legal basis.

This is not a data quality issue but a fundamental reporting difference: X reports only illegal-content decisions to the DSA register, whereas TikTok reports the vast majority of its routine policy enforcement. This has major implications for cross-platform comparisons — the datasets are measuring different enforcement populations.
